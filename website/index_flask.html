TRADE LIKE AN INSIDER

An analysis of how company insiders trade stocks


Among many participants in the US stock market, there is an unique group of investors who has knowledge and information that other investors cannot get access to. These are the company "insiders". In this study, we attempt to take a closer look into how insiders buy and selling activities. We want to answer the following question: does the possession of non-public information give insiders an edge in trading?

Start Exploring

INTRODUCTION

INSIDERS: who are they?

An insider is a person who possesses access to material and non-public information about a corporation. This makes a company's directors and high-level executive’s insiders.

While insiders only owns a small percentage of the overall equity market (see chart to the right), they remain an unique group given that the information they possess cannot be accessed with other investors.



DATA

Because of the information they have access to, insiders are required by the SEC to file prior to buying and selling stocks of which they are an insider. Also, it is required by law that insiders can only trade when they do not possess "material non-public" information. But is that truly the case?

We are using these required filings (Form 4) to do our analysis. While the data can be found on SEC website, we are using ready-cleaned data from WRDS and with supplemental data from Bloomberg.

The data includes:

  • 1791 Stocks across 12 sectors
  • 110,000 Transactions
  • 22,000 unique insiders

    OWNERSHIP OVERVIEW: Ownership varies across board

    Interesting Observations:

  • Ownership varies across sector; Consumer service has the highest ownership, utilities lowest.
  • Ownership have fluctuated over time but generally staying inside the 2-4% band. Ownership was lowest in 2013 and highest in 2015.
  • Among executives, CEOs own most shares while CFOs own least.
  • Insider ownership is highest among small cap companies (sub $5bn market cap) while lowest in large cap companies.

    NEXT CHART > Transactions

    TRANSACTIONS: More selling than buying

    Interesting Observations:

  • Insiders sell more shares than they buy, both in number of transactions and value of transactions.
  • Small Cap insiders are most active in trading; Across market caps, insiders net sell shares, with small cap insiders sell the most.
  • Insiders have generally good timing in buying/selling shares, such as selling ahead of 4Q18 crash.

    • Play with it yourself: How much shares did utilities executives buy in 3Q18 (as % of market cap)?

    NEXT CHART > Profitability

    PROFITABILITY: Not all transactions are equally profitable

    Interesting Observations:

  • Insiders purchases are more profitable than insider sales.
  • Trades tend to be most profitable 6 months from transaction date.
  • Overall insiders in healthcare are most profitable, consumer services least profitable.

    NEXT> Conclusions

    CONCLUSIONS:

    1. While insiders only own a small percentage of the overall US, the consistency in profitability of insider transactions suggest that insiders do have an edge in trading stocks.

    2. Insiders show more consistency in profitable purchases than sales. Which makes sense because there could be many reasons why insiders are selling stock (e.g. diversification, suppose of lifestyle, etc.) but there is only one reason why insiders would actively buy more shares - they expect share prices to move higher.

    3. Insiders in certain sectors, such as healthcare and tech, have more success in trading profitability than other sectors such as utilities.
